## Part I: The State of the City

### Chapter 1: The Phoenix on the Chionthar

To understand Baldur's Gate in this year of 1502 DR is to understand the nature of a scar. It is a mark of profound injury, a testament to survival, and a permanent change to the flesh upon which it is etched. A decade has passed since the skies over the city were filled with the alien flesh of a nautiloid fleet and the psychic shadow of a Netherbrain. Ten years since the city stared into the maw of ceremorphosis and did not blink. The victory, hard-won by a handful of heroes whose names are now sung in every tavern from the Elfsong to the Blushing Mermaid, was not without cost. The Absolute Crisis, as the sages now call it, was not a distant threat like the schemes of Sarevok Anchev 1 or the shadowy plot to drag the city into Avernus.1 It was an intimate violation. It walked the city's streets, wore the faces of its citizens, and whispered promises of salvation directly into the minds of the desperate.

The physical damage, though immense, is the most readily repaired. The Heapside and Eastway districts, which bore the brunt of the final conflict, are a chaotic testament to the city's furious rebirth. New stone, pale and clean, rises alongside the soot-stained, cracked facades of pre-crisis buildings. The architectural styles of Amn, Waterdeep, and even distant Sembia clash with the traditional, sturdy Baldurian designs, each new guildhall and consortium headquarters a declaration of intent from the powers that funded its construction. Cranes and scaffolding are as common a sight as gulls over the Gray Harbour, their skeletal frames a constant reminder of both the destruction and the relentless, gold-fueled recovery.

The psychological scars run deeper. Baldur's Gate has always been a city of pragmatists and survivors, a place founded by an adventurer and built on the shrewd dealings of pirates turned dukes.3 But the successive traumas of the last century and a half—from the Bhaalspawn Saga 1 to the brink of damnation during the Fall of Elturel 1—have forged a new civic identity. It is an identity defined by a survivor's paradox: an almost arrogant pride in the city's resilience, coupled with a deep, gnawing paranoia. This is a city that has looked into the abyss too many times. The result is a populace and a leadership willing to entertain any bargain, any alliance, and any sacrifice of old traditions for the promise of security and, more importantly, prosperity. The chaos of the Netherbrain's fall created a power vacuum that was filled not by heroes or patriots, but by capital. This new Baldur's Gate is a city that has, in a very real sense, been sold to the highest bidders to ensure it would not fall again. It is louder, richer, and more vibrant than ever before, but its soul now answers to the clink of foreign coin and the cold logic of the ledger book.

### Chapter 2: The Decade of Reconstruction (1492-1502 DR)

The ten years that followed the defeat of the Absolute were not a period of quiet healing, but one of frantic, often brutal, transformation. The history of this decade is the story of how Baldur's Gate became the city it is today.

#### The Immediate Aftermath (Late 1492 DR \- 1493 DR)

In the wake of the Netherbrain's destruction, Baldur's Gate was a city teetering on the edge of collapse. The ruling Council of Four was in shambles; Grand Duke Ulder Ravengard, though rescued, was politically compromised by his capture and indoctrination, and the other ducal seats were vacant or held by figures tainted by the crisis.6 The Flaming Fist, the city's primary military and police force, was decimated and its leadership discredited, leaving a vacuum of authority on the streets.7

Into this chaos poured tens of thousands of refugees. The tieflings and other displaced peoples from the Elturel region, who had already been living in squalor before the crisis, were joined by thousands more whose homes and livelihoods within Baldur's Gate had been obliterated.1 The Outer City swelled into a vast, desperate sea of shanties and tent cities, rife with disease and crime.

The heroes who had orchestrated the victory, their deeds immortalized in song, did not linger to govern. Their paths lay elsewhere: a Githyanki prince, Orpheus, returned to the Astral Plane to ignite a rebellion against the Lich-Queen Vlaakith; the heroes Karlach and Wyll descended into Avernus to wage war against the Archdevil Zariel; and others simply vanished into legend.10 They left behind a saved city that now had to save itself.

#### The Emergency Council and The Great Bargain (1493-1495 DR)

With the formal government paralyzed, an informal "Emergency Council" of influential survivors took control. This coalition was a strange mix of old and new power: remnants of the Parliament of Peers, led by shrewd patriars who had weathered the storm; the pragmatic and suddenly indispensable Guildmaster, Nine-Fingers Keene, whose network provided the only semblance of order in the Lower and Outer Cities 12; and newly ascendant merchant masters whose fortunes had, by luck or design, survived intact.

Faced with a shattered infrastructure, a bankrupt treasury, and a humanitarian crisis, the Emergency Council made a series of momentous and controversial decisions collectively known as "The Great Bargain." Unable to fund the reconstruction internally, they turned to outside powers. Emissaries were sent to the great mercantile city-states of the Sword Coast and beyond: Amn, Waterdeep, and Sembia. The offer was unprecedented: in exchange for massive, immediate infusions of gold, materials, and skilled labor to rebuild the city, these foreign powers would be granted charters to establish self-governing commercial enclaves—"Kontors"—on prime real estate within the Lower City.14 These Kontors would operate under their own laws, be protected by their own guards, and pay a fixed, long-term tax to the city treasury in lieu of normal tariffs and duties. It was a staggering concession of sovereignty, trading long-term autonomy for short-term survival. The deal was struck.

#### The Rise of the Consortiums (1495-1498 DR)

The influx of foreign capital and the hyper-competitive environment created by the Kontors forced a rapid evolution in the city's native economic structures. The old, loosely affiliated merchant guilds and trading costers like the Seven Suns and the Iron Throne, many of which had been weakened or destroyed, could not compete.16 They consolidated, merging into larger, more aggressive, and politically powerful "Merchant Consortiums." These new entities, like the Chionthar Consortium and the Western Gate Trading Company, were modeled on their foreign counterparts, with ruthless leadership and a singular focus on profit.

This economic shift was mirrored in the city's political structure. In 1497 DR, the Parliament of Peers was officially reformed. It was expanded to one hundred seats, but the nature of those seats was fundamentally altered. Fifty seats remained the hereditary right of the fifty wealthiest patriar families, a concession to tradition and the old guard.4 The other fifty, however, were designated the "Peerage of Coin." These seats were directly allocated to the largest economic entities in the city—the Consortiums, the Kontors, and the most powerful craft guilds—with the number of seats held by each faction determined by their annual tax contribution to the city. This act formally cemented Baldur's Gate as a plutocracy, where a vote in the High Hall was no longer a matter of birthright alone, but a commodity to be purchased.18

#### The Githyanki Schism and the Outer City (1496-1502 DR)

The political and economic upheaval within the walls was matched by strange new developments without. The Githyanki rebellion ignited by Prince Orpheus was not a unified movement. A significant faction of warriors, loyal to Orpheus but differing on strategy or unwilling to immediately plunge into a war against Vlaakith's might, sought refuge on the Prime Material Plane. Led by a pragmatic commander named K'laar, they established a camp in the Outer City of Baldur's Gate, a place their prince had liberated. This encampment, the "K'liir" (Gith for "Camp of the Unchained"), quickly evolved from a temporary military outpost into a permanent, bizarre enclave of astral architecture and alien technology.

Simultaneously, the refugee camps that had choked the roads to the city began to coalesce. The vast shantytown to the south, populated mainly by Elturian survivors, became a borough in its own right, known colloquially as "New Elturel." It developed its own informal governance, its own cultural identity forged in shared trauma, and a fierce, desperate piety centered on gods of hope and endurance. By 1502 DR, the Outer City was no longer a mere suburb, but a collection of distinct, powerful, and often antagonistic communities living in the shadow of Baldur's Gate proper.

## Part II: Governance, Law, and the Underbelly

### Chapter 4: The High Hall: A Republic for the Rich

The government of Baldur's Gate has always been a plutocracy, where wealth dictates influence.21 The Decade of Reconstruction did not change this fundamental truth; it merely stripped away the remaining pretenses of civic equality and formalized the dominion of coin. The city is a republic in name, but in practice, it functions as a vast mercantile corporation, with the High Hall as its boardroom and the citizenry as its workforce.

#### The Council of Four

The executive authority of Baldur's Gate remains vested in the Council of Four. However, its composition and the titles of its members have evolved to reflect the new economic realities. The title of "Grand Duke" has been replaced with the more imperious "Archduke," a position held not by a vote among the council but automatically granted to the Duke whose affiliated economic bloc contributes the most to the city's coffers in the preceding fiscal year.6 This ensures the city's chief executive is always aligned with its most powerful financial interests.

The four Dukes are no longer drawn exclusively from the ranks of the patriar families. While a prestigious name still carries weight, a seat on the council is now effectively for sale. The heads of the most powerful Merchant Consortiums and, in theory, the foreign Kontors, can and do secure ducal positions through immense financial leverage and political maneuvering within the Parliament of Peers. The Council of 1502 DR is a body of ruthless capitalists and shrewd negotiators, whose primary concern is not the welfare of the people but the health of the market. Public works are debated not on their social utility but on their return on investment. Treaties are considered not for their diplomatic implications but for the trade advantages they confer.

#### The Parliament of Peers

The Parliament of Peers, once a chamber for the city's aristocracy to advise the Dukes, has been transformed into the primary legislative battleground.4 Its expansion to 100 seats was a masterstroke of political engineering by the ascendant merchant class. The Parliament is now effectively a bicameral body, divided into two distinct, often adversarial, houses:

* The Peerage of Blood: This chamber consists of the 50 hereditary seats belonging to the oldest and wealthiest patriar families.17 They represent the "old money" and traditions of Baldur's Gate. While their direct economic power has waned compared to the new consortiums, they wield immense social and cultural influence. They see themselves as the true guardians of the city's soul, often acting as a conservative brake on the radical, profit-driven ambitions of the new elite. They fight to preserve their ancient privileges and resist the erosion of Baldurian sovereignty to foreign interests. While eligibility to sit still remains the purview of the most distinguished families, the reduced influence of these old scions of Baldur’s Gate – most directly the reduced seats reserved for the Peerage – means that confirmed citizens of every type inside the walls of the city carry a vote for who takes office. This concession has tied the interests of the Peerage to the common Balduran directly for the first time, a point of influence and pressure The Guild has been most able to take advantage of.  
* The Peerage of Coin: This chamber consists of 50 seats allocated to the city's major economic players. The number of seats a faction controls is recalculated annually based on tax receipts, creating a fiercely competitive environment. The Amnian Kontor might hold five seats one year, while the Chionthar Consortium holds seven. The Worshipful Company of Masons might have two seats, while the Vintners have one. This chamber is a cauldron of naked ambition, where laws are drafted, debated, and passed based on pure economic self-interest. Alliances are fluid, shifting with market trends and backroom deals.

Legislation must pass a vote in both Peerages before being presented to the Council of Four for ratification. This structure creates a permanent state of tension between the old aristocracy and the new plutocracy, a conflict that defines the city's high-level politics.

### Chapter 5: The Fractured Fist and the Watchful Wardens

In the new Baldur's Gate, justice is not a right but a commodity. The quality of law enforcement a citizen can expect is directly proportional to their wealth, their guild membership, and the district they inhabit. The concept of a single, unified legal authority has fractured, replaced by a patchwork of competing jurisdictions and private security forces.

#### The Flaming Fist

The once-mighty Flaming Fist mercenary company is a shadow of its former self.7 Decimated during the Absolute Crisis and politically neutered by the rise of the consortiums, the Fist has been downsized and repurposed. Its contract with the city now limits its duties to three primary areas: policing the wealthy confines of the Upper City, manning the city's outer walls, and acting as a customs enforcement agency at the Gray Harbour and the city gates.8 Funded by a dwindling share of public taxes, the Fist is chronically under-equipped and suffers from low morale. Its reputation among the general populace is abysmal; they are seen as either corrupt bullies in the employ of the patriars or as ineffective relics of a bygone era.22 Their headquarters remains the imposing Wyrm's Rock Fortress, but this serves only to highlight how far they have fallen.

#### The Guild Wardens

The true regulatory power in the Lower City's commercial and industrial sectors lies with the "Worshipful Companies." Each major guild, from the Shipwrights to the Alchemists, employs its own force of "Wardens".23 These are not mere guards but deputized agents with the authority, granted by the guild's charter, to enforce its regulations. A Guild Warden can inspect workshops for quality standards, investigate theft of trade secrets, mediate disputes between members, and mete out punishment for violations of the guild's code. Their jurisdiction is absolute over guild members and property, regardless of where they are in the city. This often leads to clashes with other authorities, but the guilds' power in the Parliament ensures their Wardens operate with near-impunity.

#### The Kontor Guards

Each of the foreign Kontors operates as a sovereign entity, and this extends to law enforcement.14 The Amnian "Coin-Swords," the Waterdhavian "Griffin Guard," and the Sembian "Iron Guard" are professional, well-armed forces responsible for security within their respective walled enclaves. They answer only to their Kontor's leadership and operate under the laws of their home nation. Any crime committed within a Kontor's walls, even by a Baldurian citizen, falls under their jurisdiction. This principle of extraterritoriality is a constant source of friction with the Flaming Fist and the Guild, creating a diplomatic minefield where a simple street brawl can escalate into an international incident.

#### The Ward Boss System

For the vast majority of citizens in the sprawling tenements of the Lower City and the shantytowns of the Outer City, none of the above authorities matter. Here, the law is The Guild. Modeled after the political machines of distant lands, Nine-Fingers Keene has divided her territory into wards, each overseen by a "Ward Boss".25 These individuals are the de facto mayors, judges, and police chiefs of their blocks. They resolve disputes, from marital squabbles to murder, through a system of swift, informal justice. They collect "taxes" in the form of protection money from every shop and household. They run "social clubs" that are fronts for gambling dens and loan-sharking operations, but also serve as community centers where a desperate family can get a bag of grain or a load of firewood in the dead of winter.27 This brutal but effective system provides a stability the official government cannot, earning The Guild a fierce loyalty from the downtrodden that no amount of gold could buy.

### Chapter 6: The Guild Ascendant

To the patriars in their Upper City manors, The Guild is a criminal syndicate, a cancer on the city's underbelly. To the common folk of the Lower City, The Guild is the government. In the decade since the crisis, Nine-Fingers Keene has orchestrated one of the most remarkable transformations in the city's history, evolving her organization from a conventional thieves' guild into a sophisticated and indispensable political machine.12

#### From Crime Lord to Political Boss

Astele "Nine-Fingers" Keene remains the undisputed Guildmaster, but her role has fundamentally changed.12 She is no longer a simple kingpin of crime; she is a political "Boss," a master of a patronage system whose sophistication rivals the political machines of cities like Waterdeep or Athkatla.26 While The Guild still profits from smuggling, extortion, and illegal games of chance, these are now merely the financial engines for its true business: the acquisition and brokerage of power. Keene herself is rarely seen in the dank sewers of the Guildhall. She maintains a respectable front, residing in a modest but heavily guarded townhouse in the Lower City, and spends her days in meetings with guild masters, consortium envoys, and even members of the Parliament of Peers who seek her favor. She understood a critical truth in the post-crisis city: the new government, obsessed with high finance and trade, had abandoned the common citizen. By providing the social safety net the state would not, she could command the loyalty of the majority of the city's population.13

#### The Machine's Structure

The modern Guild is a pyramid of power, built on patronage and loyalty.25

* **The Guildmaster (The Boss):** At the apex sits Nine-Fingers Keene, setting the overall strategy and acting as the public, if unofficial, face of the organization.  
* **The Lieutenants (The Sachems):** A small council of Keene's most trusted advisors, each responsible for overseeing a major district of the city (e.g., the Docks, Brampton, the Outskirts). They are the link between the high-level strategy and the street-level operations.  
* **The Ward Bosses (The Captains):** The heart of the machine. Each Ward Boss is responsible for a specific neighborhood or "ward." They are intimately familiar with every person, every family, and every secret in their territory. They operate out of local taverns or "social clubs" which serve as the nerve centers of the community. They are the ones who find a job for a struggling dockworker, pay for a child's medicine, or intimidate a landlord who tries to raise the rent unfairly. In return, they demand two things: information and votes. When an election for the Parliament of Peers is held, the Ward Bosses ensure their neighborhoods vote as a bloc for the Guild-approved candidate.  
* **The Rank and File (The Faithful):** The thousands of ordinary citizens who are loyal to The Guild. They are the eyes and ears of the machine, providing a constant stream of intelligence. They are the muscle for "get out the vote" efforts. They are the beneficiaries of The Guild's "honest graft"—the system of patronage that ensures loyalty through tangible rewards.

#### The Business of Power

The Guild's influence permeates every level of the city. They have informants inside the Flaming Fist, the city bureaucracy, and even the households of patriar families. This intelligence is their most valuable commodity, used for blackmail, sold to interested parties, or leveraged to gain political advantage.30 They control the city's vast smuggling networks, ensuring a steady flow of untaxed goods that both enriches their coffers and provides cheaper products for the poor. While they face competition from the ruthless Zhentarim, who deal in more overtly evil enterprises like slavery and assassination, The Guild has positioned itself as the "protector" of the common folk, a narrative that makes them far more dangerous to the established order than any simple gang of killers.13 The legitimate government in the High Hall may hold the titles, but Nine-Fingers Keene and her Guild hold the people.

## Part III: The Engine of the Gate: Economy and Commerce

### Chapter 7: The Sword Coast Hansa

The economic devastation of the Absolute Crisis paradoxically paved the way for Baldur's Gate to achieve unprecedented commercial dominance. By leveraging its strategic location on the Chionthar River and its status as the largest port on the western coast, the rebuilt city has become the undisputed center of a new economic alliance, a de facto "Sword Coast Hansa".16 This is not a formal, chartered alliance like the Lords' Alliance, but an informal yet powerful, Baldur's Gate-centric trading bloc built on standardized tariffs, shared commercial law, and mutual defense pacts with smaller cities along the coast, such as Daggerford, Beregost, and the towns of the Western Heartlands.14 The engine of this new prosperity is the constant, chaotic churn of commerce within the city itself, a system now dominated by the foreign Kontors and the powerful local consortiums they inspired.

#### The Foreign Kontors

The most striking features of the new Lower City are the four great Kontors, the physical embodiment of the "Great Bargain".34 Each is a city-within-a-city, a walled enclave of foreign architecture, culture, and law, operating with near-total autonomy on land perpetually leased to them.

* **The Amnian Kontor ("The Gold-Gable Hall"):** The largest and most opulent of the Kontors, its gilded rooftops and sandstone walls are a slice of Athkatla on the Chionthar. The Amnians dominate the city's financial sector. Their banks, backed by the wealth of Amn's powerful merchant houses, offer loans, issue letters of credit, and set the daily exchange rates for the dozens of currencies that flow through the city. They are the primary importers of southern luxuries: spices from Calimshan, rare woods from Chult, and exotic goods from the distant continent of Maztica.33 The Gold-Gable Hall is a place of immense wealth, quiet intrigue, and ruthless financial warfare.  
* **The Waterdhavian Kontor ("The Splendid Yard"):** Reflecting the grandeur of the City of Splendors, the Splendid Yard is a bastion of the Lords' Alliance and northern trade.4 Its warehouses are filled with high-quality textiles, expertly crafted metal goods, and, most importantly, magical items and components from the great workshops of Waterdeep. The Yard is also the unofficial headquarters of the Harpers and other Alliance intelligence agencies in the city, making it a hub of espionage as well as commerce.  
* **The Sembian Kontor ("The Iron Ledger"):** Utilitarian and severe, the Sembian Kontor is built of grey stone and cold efficiency. The Sembians focus on bulk goods: grain, timber, ore, and salt. They are masters of logistics and supply chain management. The Iron Ledger is also the primary hiring hall for mercenary companies in the region, brokering contracts for everyone from single adventurers to entire armies. They are known for their harsh contracts and unforgiving business practices.  
* **The Lantaner Concession ("The Cogwork Quay"):** The smallest but most technologically advanced enclave. The gnomes of Lantan operate this concession with a mixture of obsessive secrecy and dazzling public displays. It is the only legal source in the city for the volatile but effective smokepowder. The whirring and clanking of strange contraptions can be heard from behind its walls at all hours, and it is a hotbed of industrial espionage as other factions try desperately to steal its secrets.

### Chapter 8: The Worshipful Companies of Baldur's Gate

The craft guilds of Baldur's Gate have undergone a transformation as profound as the city's government. Taking inspiration from the highly structured and powerful guilds of great metropolises like Waterdeep, they have consolidated their power, becoming "Worshipful Companies".23 Each is a chartered monopoly, a social institution, and a political force in its own right. To practice a trade within the city walls without being a member of the appropriate Company is illegal and invites the swift wrath of its Wardens.

#### Structure and Power

Each Worshipful Company is a self-contained hierarchy:37

* **The Master:** The elected head of the Company, who serves a term of one to five years. The Master is the public face of the guild, sits on its boards, and often holds a seat in the Peerage of Coin.  
* **The Wardens:** Two or three senior members who act as the Master's executive officers. They oversee the Company's finances, manage its properties, and command the Guild Wardens.  
* **The Court of Assistants:** A council of a dozen or more senior masters who form the Company's governing body. They set policy, approve new members, and act as a court for internal disputes.24  
* **Masters, Journeymen, and Apprentices:** The body of the guild. An apprentice serves a seven-year term under a master to learn the trade. Upon completion, they become a journeyman, a skilled worker who can be hired by any master. To become a master oneself, a journeyman must produce a "masterpiece" to be judged by the Court of Assistants and pay a hefty fee to establish their own workshop.37

The Companies regulate every aspect of their trade: they set the prices for goods and services, enforce strict quality standards, and control the number of apprentices to prevent the labor market from being flooded. Their guildhalls are not just administrative centers but also social clubs, chapels dedicated to a patron deity, and charitable institutions that provide for sick or elderly members and their families.37

#### The Twelve Great Companies

While dozens of smaller Companies exist, twelve have risen to such prominence that they are known as the "Great Companies." Their Masters are among the most powerful individuals in the city, their collective voice capable of swaying the Council of Four.

1. **The Worshipful Company of Shipwrights & Calkers:** The most powerful guild, controlling the city's vital shipbuilding and repair industry. Their guildhall dominates the eastern docks.  
2. **The Worshipful Company of Masons & Sculptors:** Rebuilt the city after the crisis and now holds immense wealth and political power. They have a near-monopoly on all major construction projects.  
3. **The Worshipful Company of Vintners & Brewers:** Controls the lucrative trade in alcohol, from the common ale served in taverns to the fine wines imported by the Kontors.  
4. **The Worshipful Company of Mercers & Clothiers:** Works with raw textiles imported by the Kontors to produce the fine clothing worn by the city's elite.  
5. **The Worshipful Company of Goldsmiths & Jewelers:** Regulates all trade in precious metals and gems, acting as the city's unofficial mint and assay office.  
6. **The Worshipful Company of Alchemists & Apothecaries:** A powerful and secretive guild that controls the production and sale of potions, antitoxins, and more exotic substances. Their rivalry with the Poisoners' Guild is legendary.  
7. **The Worshipful Company of Fishmongers:** An ancient and influential guild that controls the vast fishing fleets operating out of Gray Harbour.  
8. **The Worshipful Company of Smiths & Armorers:** Forges the weapons and armor that equip the city's many guards and adventurers. Dammon's "Forge of the Nine" is a prominent, though independent-minded, member establishment.40  
9. **The Worshipful Company of Cartographers & Surveyors:** Essential for a city built on trade and property. They map new trade routes and their seal is required on every land deed in the city.  
10. **The Worshipful Company of Butchers & Tanners:** A wealthy but socially looked-down-upon guild that controls the city's food supply and leather production.  
11. **The Worshipful Company of Chandlers & Lamplighters:** Provides the oil, candles, and torches that light the city, a vital and consistently profitable enterprise.  
12. **The Sorcerous Sodality:** Not a traditional Company, but the reformed Sorcerous Sundries has been granted a similar charter. It regulates the sale of magical scrolls and components and the licensing of spellcasting services, making it one of the most feared and respected bodies in Baldur's Gate.

### Chapter 9: The Price of Life

For all its high politics and grand economics, the life of an average Baldurian is governed by the simple realities of cost and currency. The city runs on gold, silver, and copper, and the price of survival varies dramatically from the glittering spires of the Upper City to the muddy lanes of the Outskirts. The following provides a practical guide to the cost of goods, services, and living in Baldur's Gate, based on extensive research in the city's markets and halls.42

#### Lifestyle Expenses

The cost of living in Baldur's Gate is a direct reflection of its stratified society. These daily costs cover lodging, food, and basic maintenance.45

* **Wretched (Free \- 1 sp/day):** This is the life of a beggar in the Outer City or a sewer-dweller in the Undercity. Shelter is a leaky lean-to or a shared patch of floor in a flophouse. Food is whatever can be scavenged or begged.  
* **Squalid (1-2 sp/day):** The standard for an Elturian refugee in New Elturel or an unemployed laborer. This affords a cot in a crowded tenement dormitory and a daily meal of thin gruel or stale bread.  
* **Poor (2-5 sp/day):** The life of a common dockworker or apprentice. This might secure a small, shared room in a Lower City tenement and two simple meals a day. It is a life of constant work with little left over.  
* **Modest (1-2 gp/day):** A journeyman craftsman, a Flaming Fist private, or a clerk in a merchant house lives this way. This lifestyle provides a private (if small) room, decent food, and the ability to afford a mug of ale at a tavern like the Elfsong Tavern.48 A small house in the Lower City might cost between 2,000 and 5,000 gold pieces, but renting is far more common.49  
* **Comfortable (8-15 gp/day):** A successful merchant, a junior officer in the Fist, or a skilled professional. This affords a small apartment or townhouse in a better part of the Lower City, fine clothes, and regular entertainment.  
* **Wealthy (50-100 gp/day):** The lifestyle of a Guild Master, a senior Kontor official, or a minor patriar. This includes a spacious home in the Upper City or a large Lower City townhouse with a few servants, fine dining, and significant disposable income.  
* **Aristocratic (250+ gp/day):** The province of the great patriar lords and the heads of the major consortiums. A manor in the Upper City with a full staff of servants, private guards, the finest food and wine, and near-limitless political and social influence.

## Part IV: The City and Its People

### Chapter 10: A Melting Pot on the Sword Coast

The population of Baldur's Gate has exploded in the last decade. From a pre-crisis population of roughly 125,000, the city and its immediate environs now house an estimated 145,000 souls, making it one of the most populous and diverse metropolises in all of Faerûn.21 This demographic shift has created a city of vibrant cultural exchange and deep social friction. The old, simple conflict between the wealthy Upper City and the poor Lower City has been complicated by new fault lines of ancestry, nationality, and ideology.

#### Ancestry and Community Distribution

While humans remain the dominant ancestry, composing roughly 70% of the population, the city is a true melting pot.52 The remaining 30% is a complex tapestry of peoples from across the continent and beyond.

* **Humans (approx. 70%):** The human population is no longer monolithic. There is a clear social stratification between the native **Baldurians**, who view the city as their birthright; the **Elturians**, refugees from the fallen city who now form a large, cohesive community in the Outer City; and the **Foreign Nationals** (primarily Amnians, Waterdhavians, and Sembians) who live and work in the Kontors and often hold themselves apart from the local populace.  
* **Tieflings (approx. 8%):** Once a small minority, the tiefling population swelled dramatically with the arrival of the Elturian refugees.1 Concentrated in the "New Elturel" district of the Outer City, they have formed a strong, self-reliant community. They are a potent political force, often courted by The Guild and viewed with suspicion by nativist Baldurians.  
* **Dwarves (approx. 6%):** A large and well-established dwarven community has long been a cornerstone of the city's craft industries. They are heavily concentrated in the Masons', Smiths', and Goldsmiths' Companies, and their reputation for diligence and honesty makes them highly sought after as artisans and bankers.54  
* **Elves and Half-Elves (approx. 5%):** A stable and integrated population, elves and half-elves can be found at all levels of society, from patriar families with ancient lineages to merchants, artisans, and scholars in the Lower City.54 Many hold key positions in the Waterdhavian Kontor and the Sorcerous Sodality.  
* **Halflings and Gnomes (approx. 4%):** These smaller folk are a common sight in the city's markets and taverns. Halflings are often publicans, merchants, and members of The Guild's smuggling operations, while gnomes are prominent as tinkers, alchemists, and artificers, with many drawn to the Lantaner Concession.54  
* **Githyanki (approx. 1%):** The newest and most alien addition to the city's demographic. The Githyanki of the K'liir are a warrior society living in self-imposed exile. They remain aloof from most city affairs but are a source of immense curiosity and fear. Their advanced martial skills and strange psionic abilities make them formidable mercenaries, and their knowledge of the Astral Plane is a commodity sought by powerful wizards and scholars.10  
* **Others (approx. 6%):** The great port attracts all kinds. Dragonborn, half-orcs, and even the occasional reformed goblin or kobold can be found in the city's margins, typically working as laborers, mercenaries, or sailors, finding a degree of anonymity in the urban sprawl that they would be denied elsewhere.55

## Part V: Factions and Power Brokers

### Chapter 12: Directory of Influence

The true power in Baldur's Gate is not held by a single entity but is contested by a multitude of factions, each with its own agenda, resources, and sphere of influence. Understanding this complex web of alliances, rivalries, and secret societies is the key to navigating the city's treacherous political landscape.

#### The Council of Four & Parliament of Peers

* **Goals & Influence:** The official, legitimate government of Baldur's Gate, responsible for creating and executing laws. In reality, its goal is to maintain the city's economic growth and the status quo that benefits its members. Its influence is absolute in theory but fractured in practice. The Council of Four, led by the Archduke, holds executive power, while the Parliament of Peers drafts legislation. 20 The entire structure is a plutocracy; power is wielded by those with the most coin, whether from ancient patriar lineage (the Peerage of Blood) or from raw commercial success (the Peerage of Coin). 18  
* **Structure:** The Council of Four are the chief executives, their ranks filled by the most powerful players in the Parliament. The Parliament itself is a 100-seat body divided between the 50 hereditary patriar families and 50 seats allocated to the largest economic entities in the city. 4 Their meetings in the High Hall are a constant battle between the conservative, tradition-bound patriars and the aggressive, profit-driven consortiums and Kontors. 24  
* **Notable Members:** The ducal seats are in constant flux, but currently, **Archduke Lyra Silvershield** of the Western Gate Trading Company holds the top position. Other notable figures include the elderly but still sharp **Lord Corin Durinbold**, a leading voice in the Peerage of Blood, and **Consul Vorlamin Athar** of the Amnian Kontor, a powerful player in the Peerage of Coin. 6  
* **Key Relationships:** The Council and Parliament are a web of fragile alliances. They are officially in command of the Flaming Fist but lack the funds to make it truly effective. They are openly hostile to The Guild's influence but are often forced to negotiate with it to maintain order. Their primary relationship is with money; they are beholden to the consortiums and foreign powers that fund the city's treasury and, by extension, their own power.

#### The Flaming Fist

* **Goals & Influence:** Once the mighty military and police force of Baldur's Gate, the Flaming Fist's primary goal is now simple survival and the hope of regaining its lost prestige. 1 Its influence is a shadow of its former self, largely confined to policing the Upper City, manning the outer walls, and collecting customs at the gates and harbor. 8 To the patriars, they are private security; to the common folk, they are corrupt and ineffective. 22  
* **Structure:** A strict military hierarchy remains, but it is a hollowed-out version of its past glory. The **Marshal** leads the company, followed by **Blazes** (logistics/personnel), **Flames** (field commanders), **Manips** (unit commanders), and **Fists** (privates). 3 The organization is headquartered at the formidable Wyrm's Rock Fortress, a symbol of strength that no longer reflects reality. 58  
* **Notable Members:** Following the political fallout from Grand Duke Ravengard's capture and subsequent death, the position of Marshal has been unstable. The current Marshal is **Valerius Flint**, a grim, by-the-book veteran who rose from the ranks. He is respected by his soldiers but lacks the political cunning to navigate the city's new landscape. **Blaze Liara Portyr** is a notable and ambitious officer from a powerful family, often seen as a potential successor to Flint. 1  
* **Key Relationships:** The Fist has a contentious relationship with all other armed groups. They resent the well-funded private guards of the Kontors and the jurisdictional authority of the Guild Wardens. They view The Guild as their primary antagonist but lack the resources and political will to challenge it directly in the Lower and Outer Cities. They are begrudgingly allied with the Lords' Alliance when their interests align but are generally seen as a purely local, and diminished, power. 1

#### The Guild

* **Goals & Influence:** The Guild's goal is the complete and total control of the Lower and Outer Cities, not through overt rule, but through a deeply embedded system of patronage and populism. 25 Its influence is immense, far surpassing that of the Flaming Fist in the areas it controls. It is the de facto government for the common folk, providing services, justice, and a social safety net that the official government has abandoned. 13  
* **Structure:** The Guild has evolved from a simple criminal syndicate into a sophisticated political machine. At the top is **Guildmaster Nine-Fingers Keene**. 5 Below her are her **Lieutenants** (or "Sachems"), who oversee large districts. The core of the operation are the **Ward Bosses** ("Captains"), who control individual neighborhoods, providing aid and enforcing loyalty. 25 The entire structure is supported by thousands of loyal citizens who act as informants and enforcers. Its public-facing operations are run from various social clubs and taverns, but its true headquarters is the well-defended  
  **Guildhall** in the Undercity sewers. 5  
* **Notable Members:** **Astele "Nine-Fingers" Keene** is the undisputed leader, a brilliant strategist and political operator. 27 Her inner circle includes figures like  
  **"Farlin,"** the ruthless Ward Boss of the Eastway smuggling routes, and **Tusgront,** the hulking enforcer who maintains order near the Basilisk Gate. 12 The tiefling orphan **Mol** is a rising star, managing a network of young informants and pickpockets with surprising efficiency. 12  
* **Key Relationships:** The Guild's primary rival is the **Zhentarim**, whom they see as reckless and destructive interlopers. 2 They have a complex, adversarial relationship with the **Flaming Fist** and the **Council of Four**, often engaging in backroom deals to maintain a careful balance of power. They have a working, if wary, relationship with the **Harpers**, who recognize The Guild's role in maintaining a certain kind of stability. 2 The Guild is the patron of the common folk and thus has a positive relationship with the city's downtrodden masses.

#### The Foreign Kontors (Amn, Waterdeep, Sembia, Lantan)

* **Goals & Influence:** The four foreign enclaves—the Amnian, Waterdhavian, Sembian, and Lantaner—are powerful economic factions whose primary goal is to exploit the markets of Baldur's Gate for the benefit of their home nations. Their influence is immense, particularly in the Peerage of Coin, where their vast wealth buys them significant political power. They control entire sectors of the city's economy: Amnian banking, Waterdhavian magical trade, Sembian bulk goods, and Lantaner technology. 14  
* **Structure:** Each Kontor is a sovereign, walled enclave within the Lower City, governed by its own leader (an Amnian Consul, a Waterdhavian Factor, etc.) and protected by its own private military force. They operate under the laws of their home nation, a principle of extraterritoriality that is a constant source of friction. 14  
* **Notable Members:** **Consul Vorlamin Athar** of Amn is a master of financial warfare. **Factor Elara Mornwood** of Waterdeep is a high-ranking member of the Lords' Alliance and a shrewd intelligence operative. **Bursar Korgan Ironhand**, a dwarf, runs the Sembian Kontor with ruthless efficiency. **Chief Artificer Glim-Nimblewhiz** jealously guards the secrets of the Lantaner Concession.  
* **Key Relationships:** The Kontors are in constant, fierce competition with each other and with the local **Chionthar Consortium**. They bribe **Council** members and use their economic leverage to influence city policy. They have a purely transactional relationship with **The Guild**, using its smuggling services when it suits them.

#### The Chionthar Consortium & Western Gate Trading Company

* **Goals & Influence:** These are the two largest native Baldurian merchant consortiums, formed by local guilds and patriar families to compete with the foreign Kontors. The Chionthar Consortium seeks to monopolize all river trade, while the Western Gate Trading Company aims to control the overland caravan routes. Their influence is primarily economic, and they are major players in the Peerage of Coin, often forming a voting bloc against foreign interests.  
* **Structure:** Both are structured like modern corporations, with a Master or Matron at the head of a board of directors drawn from the most powerful member families and guilds. Their headquarters are opulent halls in the Lower and Upper Cities.  
* **Notable Members:** **Master Joric the Stout**, a gruff former river captain, leads the Chionthar Consortium. **Matron Lyra Silvershield**, a cunning patriar, is the current head of the Western Gate Trading Company and the sitting Archduke.  
* **Key Relationships:** Their primary rivals are the **Foreign Kontors**. They are in a constant state of economic warfare, using everything from price manipulation to industrial sabotage. They are allied with the **Patriar Heritage Society** in their desire to protect Baldurian interests, but often clash over methods.

#### The Harpers

* **Goals & Influence:** The Harpers are a secretive organization dedicated to preserving balance and combating tyranny. In Baldur’s Gate, their goal is to monitor the city’s many powers and prevent any one faction from endangering the populace or the stability of the region. They thrive on information gathering and subtle intervention. Their influence is mostly hidden but surprisingly deep, with a network of spies in taverns, docks, and even among patriar households. Historically, Harpers were pivotal in thwarting major threats to the city. 47  
* **Structure:** The Harpers have no formal base. They use safehouses and the cover of existing establishments. **Danthelon's Dancing Axe** on Wyrm's Crossing is a known front. 48 The local cell is loosely organized, answering to regional leadership but operating with significant autonomy. The current cell leader is believed to be **"The Nightingale,"** a successor to the legendary Jaheira, who coordinates agents via coded messages and clandestine meetings.  
* **Notable Members:** The legendary ranger **Jaheira** was openly involved in defeating the Absolute cult, and her legacy inspires the local chapter. 2  
  **Randal Evenwood**, a half-elf bard posing as a fencing instructor for patriar youths, is a key agent. **Tilly Falkenrath**, a young tiefling bard, gathers gossip in the salons of the elite. **Nahil Morovar**, a human magistrate, is believed to be a source for information on corruption within the legal system.  
* **Key Relationships:** The Harpers are enemies of the **Zhentarim** and the various dark cults (Bhaal, Shar, Absolute remnants). They maintain a cautious, sometimes cooperative relationship with **The Guild**, seeing it as a necessary evil that keeps worse elements in check. 2 They are wary of the **Flaming Fist's** corruption and the **Council's** plutocratic overreach, often leaking information to the **Baldur's Mouth** to shape public opinion. They cooperate with agents of the **Lords' Alliance** within the Waterdhavian Kontor when their goals align.

#### The Zhentarim

* **Goals & Influence:** The Zhentarim, or Black Network, are a ruthless mercenary and criminal organization driven by a single motive: profit. Their goal in Baldur's Gate is to supplant The Guild and seize control of the city's lucrative underworld, including smuggling, extortion, and the trade of illegal goods like slaves and poisons. 2 Their influence is insidious and growing, built on fear, bribery, and a willingness to work with anyone, including the forces of the Absolute during the recent crisis. 2  
* **Structure:** The Zhentarim operate in clandestine cells, lacking the public-facing structure of The Guild. Their operations are compartmentalized to prevent a single captured agent from compromising the entire network. They use legitimate-seeming businesses as fronts and maintain hidden bases throughout the Lower and Outer Cities.  
* **Notable Members:** The local operation is overseen by the cunning and ambitious trader **Roah Moonglow**, who has proven adept at playing all sides. 29 She commands a cadre of enforcers and assassins, known for their brutal efficiency.  
* **Key Relationships:** The Zhentarim are in a state of cold war with **The Guild**, their primary obstacle to controlling the city's crime. They are natural enemies of the **Harpers**, who actively work to disrupt their schemes. They view the **Flaming Fist** and the **Council** as corrupt institutions to be bribed and manipulated, not as legitimate authorities. They will happily make temporary alliances with any other faction, from foreign Kontors to dark cults, if it serves their bottom line. 29

#### The Unchained (K'liir)

* **Goals & Influence:** The primary goal of the Githyanki exiles of the K'liir is to survive and prepare. They are a splinter faction of Prince Orpheus's rebellion, dedicated to his cause but choosing to gather strength on the Prime Material Plane before challenging the Lich-Queen Vlaakith directly. Their influence in the city is minimal by choice, but their presence is a powerful one. They are a source of unparalleled martial prowess, psionic power, and knowledge of the Astral Plane, making them a valuable but dangerous resource for those brave enough to seek them out. 10  
* **Structure:** The K'liir is a military encampment organized along Githyanki warrior lines. It is led by a single, battle-tested commander who maintains strict discipline. Their society is communal and entirely focused on martial and psionic training. Their enclave in the Outer City is a bizarre and unsettling landmark of alien architecture.  
* **Notable Members:** The founder and commander of the K'liir is **K'laar, the Unshackled**, a pragmatic and fiercely loyal follower of Orpheus. She is a veteran of countless battles in the Astral Plane and commands the absolute respect of her warriors.  
* **Key Relationships:** The Unchained remain aloof from most city politics. They have a tense but generally non-hostile relationship with the **Hellriders of New Elturel**, their closest neighbors. They are occasionally hired as elite mercenaries by the **Sembian Kontor** or wealthy **Patriars**, but their loyalty is only to their contract. They are utterly hostile to any hint of mind flayer activity, making them enemies of the **Hand of the Absolute**.

#### Hellriders of New Elturel

* **Goals & Influence:** This faction is the informal government and militia of the New Elturel district in the Outer City. Their primary goal is the protection and welfare of the Elturian refugee population. They lobby constantly for official recognition, aid, and citizenship from the Baldurian government, making them a potent political force due to their large, unified population.  
* **Structure:** A volunteer militia led by a Commander, structured loosely on the old Hellrider hierarchy of Elturel. They patrol New Elturel and operate out of the High Rider's Stand, a large pavilion that serves as their headquarters and town hall.  
* **Notable Members:** Led by **High Watcher Alena**, a priestess of Torm who has become a respected community organizer and spiritual guide.  
* **Key Relationships:** They have a deep-seated mistrust of the **Council of Four** and the **Flaming Fist**, whom they see as having abandoned them. They have a working relationship with **The Guild**, which provides aid and protection that the official city does not. They are wary of their strange neighbors, **The Unchained**, but maintain a respectful distance.

#### The Dark Cults (Bhaal, Shar, Absolute)

* **Goals & Influence:** Though the great public threats have been defeated, the dark gods' influence lingers. The **Cult of the Returning Lord** (Bhaal) seeks to empower their resurrected god through ritual murder, operating from hidden shrines in the Undercity. The **Silent Shroud** (Shar) has re-established a presence in the House of Grief, working to spread despair and undermine the city's temples of light. The **Hand of the Absolute** are scattered, leaderless cells of True Soul survivors who dream of avenging the Netherbrain and re-establishing a mind flayer colony. Their influence is secretive and insidious, preying on the desperate and disenfranchised.  
* **Structure:** These are all clandestine cell-based organizations. They lack central leadership and operate in the shadows, making them difficult to eradicate.  
* **Notable Members:** Leaders are unknown, operating under aliases. Any charismatic individual who survived the Absolute Crisis could potentially lead a cell of the Hand. Any sufficiently nihilistic noble could be a patron of the Silent Shroud.  
* **Key Relationships:** They are enemies of all other factions, particularly the **Harpers** and the city's official religious orders. They may secretly try to infiltrate other groups, like **The Guild** or even the **Flaming Fist**, to further their aims.

#### The Worshipful Companies

* **Goals & Influence:** The twelve Great Companies (and the dozens of lesser ones) are the backbone of the city's economy. Their primary goal is to maintain their chartered monopoly over their respective trades, setting prices, controlling quality, and limiting competition. Their collective influence is enormous, especially within the Peerage of Coin, where the Masters of the Great Companies hold significant power.  
* **Structure:** Each Company is a rigid hierarchy of Master, Wardens, Court of Assistants, masters, journeymen, and apprentices. 37 They operate from grand Guildhalls scattered throughout the Lower City.  
* **Notable Members:** **Master Helma Tallowshand** of the Shipwrights' Company can bring the harbor to a standstill. **Master Borin Stonehand** of the Masons' Company grew immensely rich and powerful from the city's reconstruction. The unnamed Master of the **Sorcerous Sodality** (based in Sorcerous Sundries) regulates all public magic use with an iron fist.  
* **Key Relationships:** The Companies are in constant competition with each other for influence and resources. They are often at odds with **The Guild** over untaxed labor and smuggled goods. They have a complex relationship with the **Foreign Kontors**, both as business partners and as rivals.

#### The Baldur's Mouth

* **Goals & Influence:** The city's primary news-sheet, its goal is simple: profit. It achieves this by selling papers and, more importantly, by selling its influence. The Baldur's Mouth can shape public opinion, ruin reputations, or promote a political agenda for any faction with enough gold to pay its editor.  
* **Structure:** A single, chaotic print shop in the Lower City.  
* **Notable Members:** **Editor Ettvard Needle**, a man with no morals but an excellent sense of what sells.  
* **Key Relationships:** The Mouth has a relationship with every faction in the city, all of them transactional. It is a tool to be used by the **Council**, the **Guild**, the **Kontors**, and the **Harpers** alike to plant stories and sway public discourse.

#### Knights of the Shield

* **Goals & Influence:** A once-powerful secret society of merchants and nobles who bargained with devils for power, the Knights were shattered during the Absolute Crisis. Their goal is now to rebuild their network and regain their influence over the city's elite. Their influence is currently minimal, but their knowledge of diabolism and their hidden cells make them a potential threat.  
* **Structure:** A scattered network of secret cells, communicating through coded messages and meeting in forgotten cellars. They are led by an unknown "Hidden Lord."  
* **Key Relationships:** They are enemies of the **Harpers** and any devoutly good-aligned factions. They may seek alliances of convenience with the **Zhentarim** or corrupt **Patriars**.

#### The River-Rats & The Wharf Rats

* **Goals & Influence:** These are two distinct but related sub-factions of The Guild. The River-Rats are a specialized smuggling ring, led by **"Silas the Eel,"** who use the city's sewer tunnels and hidden river coves to bypass Flaming Fist customs. The Wharf Rats are a Guild-controlled dockworkers' union, led by **"One-Eyed" Jack,** used to control labor at the port, gather intelligence, and facilitate the movement of contraband off ships. Their influence is localized to the docks and waterways but is critical to The Guild's smuggling operations.  
* **Structure:** Loosely organized gangs that answer directly to a Guild Lieutenant.  
* **Key Relationships:** They are extensions of **The Guild** and share its friends and enemies. They are in constant conflict with the **Flaming Fist** customs patrols and the **Worshipful Company of Shipwrights**, who resent their control over the dockworkers.

#### Patriar Heritage Society

* **Goals & Influence:** This is not a formal organization but a powerful political bloc of "old money" patriar families from the Peerage of Blood. 4 Their primary goal is to preserve their traditional rights, privileges, and social standing against the rising tide of the "new money" plutocrats in the Peerage of Coin. They seek to roll back the reforms of the Great Bargain and restore the primacy of birthright over naked commerce in the city's politics. Their influence stems from their ancient names, vast (if sometimes dwindling) fortunes, and deep-rooted connections within the city's institutions. 6  
* **Structure:** The society is an informal alliance of families who vote and act as a bloc in the Parliament of Peers. Their meetings are held not in a public hall, but in the private salons and dining rooms of their Upper City manors. 4  
* **Notable Members:** The de facto leader of the traditionalist faction is **Lord Corin "the Elder" Durinbold**, a proud and stubborn man who views the foreign Kontors as a cancer on the city. 6 Other key families in the alliance include the  
  **Jannath**, **Hlath**, and **Oberon** houses. 6  
* **Key Relationships:** They are the primary political opponents of the **Merchant Consortiums** and the **Foreign Kontors**. They view the **Council of Four**, currently dominated by new money, with contempt. They are the primary patrons of the **Flaming Fist**, seeing the mercenary company as a symbol of the old Baldurian order. They have a deep disdain for **The Guild**, viewing it as a symptom of the city's decay, yet are not above using its services for blackmail or information. 5

#### Free Traders of the Outskirts

* **Goals & Influence:** An unofficial merchant's guild for the caravan masters, independent artisans, and shopkeepers of the Outer City. Their goal is to protect their members from the predatory practices of both the city's official guilds and The Guild's extortion rackets. Their influence is confined to the Outer City but is strong there, as they provide a unified front for the thousands living outside the walls.  
* **Structure:** A loose cooperative led by a council of the most respected merchants. They operate out of the Wyrm's Bend Market.  
* **Notable Members:** **"Mad" Meggan**, a tough-as-nails caravan master who has survived a dozen bandit attacks and isn't afraid of anyone from inside the walls.  
* **Key Relationships:** They are in constant conflict with the **Flaming Fist** over tolls and tariffs at the city gates. They resist the encroachment of **The Guild's** protection rackets. They are natural allies of the **Hellriders of New Elturel** and other Outer City communities.

#### Circle of the Inner Grove

* **Goals & Influence:** A small, secretive druidic sect dedicated to protecting the Chionthar River and the surrounding lands from the city's pollution and expansion. Their influence is subtle, using nature's power to sabotage industrial sites that pollute too heavily or to guide dangerous creatures toward those who would harm the natural balance.  
* **Structure:** A small circle of druids and rangers who meet in a hidden grove upriver from the city.  
* **Notable Members:** **Elder Elion**, a wizened half-elf druid who remembers when the Chionthar ran clear.  
* **Key Relationships:** They are hostile to the **Worshipful Company of Butchers & Tanners** and other industrial guilds. They are wary of all the city factions but may find common cause with the **Harpers** on specific issues.

#### The Remnant Costers (Iron Throne, Seven Suns, High Moon)

* **Goals & Influence:** These are the ghosts of the city's old economic order. The Iron Throne, the Seven Suns, and the High Moon Coster were once great powers, but they were shattered by the Bhaalspawn crisis and the rise of the new consortiums. Their goal is simply to survive. They have carved out small niches to stay afloat: the Iron Throne deals in high-risk, high-reward mercenary work; the Seven Suns focuses on rare, luxury goods; and the High Moon Coster finances adventuring expeditions for a share of the profits. Their influence is minimal.  
* **Structure:** Each is run by the descendants of the original families, operating out of their remaining manors in the Upper City.  
* **Notable Members:** The **Jhasso** and **Oberon** families still cling to the leadership of the Seven Suns and High Moon Costers, respectively. The leadership of the Iron Throne is a shadowy, anonymous figure.  
* **Key Relationships:** They are largely irrelevant to the major power struggles but may serve as patrons or sources of information for adventurers. They hold a deep grudge against the new **Merchant Consortiums** that supplanted them.

#### Followers of the Phoenix

* **Goals & Influence:** A new and growing street-level cult that venerates the hero Karlach. They interpret her survival and journey into Avernus as a holy act of righteous fury and self-sacrifice. Their goal is to spread her story and offer a path of redemption and purpose to the downtrodden and the outcast. Their influence is small but growing, particularly among the city's tiefling population and those who feel left behind by the new order.  
* **Structure:** A charismatic leader, known only as **"The Spark,"** preaches from a small, makeshift shrine in a forgotten corner of the Lower City. It is a grassroots movement with no formal hierarchy.  
* **Key Relationships:** They are viewed with suspicion by the established temples and the **Flaming Fist**, who see them as a potential source of civil unrest. They are generally on good terms with the **Hellriders of New Elturel**.

#### The Bibliophile & The Gravekeepers

* **Goals & Influence:** These two factions represent the city's hidden worlds of information and secrets. **The Bibliophile**, a seemingly legitimate bookstore in Bloomridge run by **Nansi Gretta**, is actually one of the city's most sophisticated spy networks, selling information to the highest bidder. **The Gravekeepers**, operating out of Candulhallow's Tombstones and led by **"Old Man Hemlock,"** are a secret society dedicated to protecting the city from the undead threats that inevitably arise from its many ancient crypts and graveyards. Both factions have deep, hidden influence in their respective spheres.  
* **Structure:** Both are small, secretive organizations whose members are known only to each other.  
* **Key Relationships:** The Bibliophile is neutral, selling information to all sides, including the **Council**, **The Guild**, and the **Harpers**. The Gravekeepers are solitary but may cooperate with any faction, even the **Flaming Fist**, when a major undead threat emerges.

## Part VI: A Walker's Guide to the Gate

### Chapter 13: The Lay of the Land

To navigate Baldur's Gate is to understand its verticality and its walls. The city is built on and around a great bluff on the north bank of the Chionthar River, just a few days' sail from the Sea of Swords. 16 This geography creates the city's three great divisions: the Upper, Lower, and Outer Cities.

* **The Upper City:** Atop the bluff sits the Upper City, the domain of the wealthy patriar class. 60 It is entirely enclosed by its own formidable, magically-lit stone walls. Access is tightly controlled by the Flaming Fist at a handful of gates, separating it from the common folk below.  
* **The Lower City:** Spilling down the steep slopes from the Upper City's wall to the Gray Harbour is the Lower City, the city's beating commercial heart. 60 This crescent-shaped sprawl is a chaotic maze of slate-roofed tenements, workshops, and guildhalls, all packed together and crisscrossed by bridges and buttresses to keep them from sliding into the harbor. 60 The Lower City is itself walled, with the ancient "Old Wall" separating it from the Upper City and a newer, formidable outer wall protecting it from the lands beyond.  
* **The Outer City:** Everything outside the main walls is considered the Outer City. This is not a single entity, but a sprawling collection of towns, shantytowns, and camps that have grown up along the trade roads and the river. 61 The most significant portion lies south of the Chionthar, connected to the city proper by the great Wyrm's Crossing bridge. 48

Movement between these areas is defined by the city's gates. The most significant are:

* **The Gates of the Upper City:** These gates, such as the one leading from the High Hall to the Lower City, are strictly policed by the Flaming Fist and serve as a hard border between the classes.  
* **Baldur's Gate:** The oldest and most famous gate, piercing the Old Wall. It is the primary artery for commerce and people moving between the Upper and Lower Cities, a constant symbol of the city's class divide. 60  
* **Basilisk Gate:** The main eastern gate in the outer wall, opening onto the Coast Way trade road. It is the primary entry point for overland caravans from the south and east. 60  
* **Cliffgate:** A smaller, fog-shrouded western gate leading to the Tumbledown district and its graveyards. It has a grim reputation for both hauntings and muggings. 60

### Chapter 14: The Upper City: An Island of Order

The Upper City is a world apart, a haven of wealth, beauty, and political power. 60 Its clean, magically-lit streets are lined with historic manors, exclusive boutiques, and grand temples. Here, the patriar families and the new money elite live in splendid isolation, their primary concerns being social standing and political maneuvering. The Watch (a division of the Flaming Fist) provides a level of security unimaginable in the districts below, but the greatest dangers often come from the whispered intrigues and backroom deals made within the district's own gilded halls. 62

#### Manorborn

**Overview.**  
If prestige had an address, it would be here. Manorborn is a quiet world of walled gardens, hushed courtyards, and manors whose names are older than most nations. Streets are broad and freshly swept; door knockers gleam; the air smells faintly of citrus and beeswax. It is beautiful, theatrical—and very aware that you are visiting.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Uniformed house guards at every gate, servant bell-pulls chiming behind ivy, coach wheels whispering over clean stone. Beggars and hawkers are politely redirected at the Old Wall; the noise of the Lower City seems to stop at the lintel.

**Notable stops.**

- **The Helm and Cloak.** An invitation-only club and inn where civic gossip wears perfume. Dining is exquisite; politics even more so.  
- **Watchful Shield Shrine (Helm).** Marble, brass lamps, and even brassier discretion. The Watch and well-heeled petitioners come for blessings that double as alibis.  
- **Patriar Garden Walks.** On festival eves, a handful of estates open their front gardens for strolling. You won’t see their vaults—but you’ll see who’s courting whom.  
- **The Limehouse Conservatory.** A glass-roofed gallery of imported fruit trees and private recitals; tickets are scarce and status is the point.

**Who lives here.**  
Old families with older ledgers; new fortunes buying old manners; stewards, legists, and armies of staff who keep the façade flawless.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Dress neatly, sheath blades, speak softly. Entry after dusk typically requires papers or a resident sponsor. Guards are polite until you give them a reason not to be.

**Local pressures.**  
Inheritance feuds, fortunes stretched thinner than the plaster, and the kind of crimes that leave no bruises: blackmail, quiet embezzlement, and reputations arranged like chess pieces.

#### The Temples District

**Overview.**  
This is the Upper City’s public face: bell towers, broad steps, and processions threading past government façades. Piety and policy share the same boulevard; salvation and permits are both stamped here.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Incense drifting from open doors; clerks with armfuls of scrolls; queues that move with ritual exactness. On holy days, the district blooms with banners and festival stalls approved by three different offices.

**Notable stops.**

- **High Hall.** Council chambers, public hearings, and a civic museum’s worth of triumphal murals. Arrive early if you plan to petition.  
- **High House of Wonders & the Hall of Wonders (Gond).** Part workshop, part marvel-hall. Expect whirring exhibits, guided tours, and a tasteful donation box that is never empty.  
- **Lady’s Hall (Tymora).** Bright, busy, and lucky by design. Merchants and adventurers come here to hedge fate before big ventures.  
- **Stormshore Tabernacle.** A many-faith sanctuary with rotating rites. Priests debate theology in the morning and share bread recipes in the afternoon.

**Who lives here.**  
Prelates, advocates, archivists, and functionaries who can recite bylaws by heart. Pilgrims and petitioners swell the crowds from dawn to dusk.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Peace-tie weapons. Expect small offerings at most shrines and a larger one if you are asking for anything remarkable. Lines are long, but impatience earns you nothing.

**Local pressures.**  
Competition between temples for endowments; relic thefts smoothed over to avoid scandal; bureaucratic turf wars where a misplaced seal can stall a life for weeks.

#### The Wide

**Overview.**  
The city’s grand market square is a disciplined riot by day and a stage set by night. Licensed stalls unfurl at first bell—silks, spices, spell-components, and notarized contracts—then fold away at dusk, leaving a lantern-lit emptiness that makes your steps sound very loud.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Tidy rows of awnings; city clerks with stamp boxes; professional hawkers who can read your purse from twenty paces. The Watch keeps order with visible calm and invisible efficiency.

**Notable stops.**

- **Licensed Stalls & Factors’ Arcades.** The best place “above the Wall” to compare wares without risking your skin. Haggling is expected; receipts are essential.  
- **The Weighhouse.** Public scales where disputes are settled in the open; the scale’s honesty is legendary and loudly defended.  
- **Auction Rostrum.** Estate lots, ship cargo overages, the occasional “miscellany of interest.” Come early for a seat and later for rumors.  
- **Discrete Stairwells.** Locals swear a few colonnades conceal steps to the **Undercellar**. You didn’t hear it from this guide.

**Who lives here.**  
Few. The Wide is for business. By evening, only resident shopkeepers, sweepers, and patrols remain.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Only licensed sellers may trade here. Keep purses close, papers closer, and an eye on closing bell; after dark the square is cleared with brisk efficiency.

**Local pressures.**  
License disputes, counterfeit seals, and “helpful” middlemen who attach themselves to your purchases like barnacles. After-hours deliveries pass unnoticed until they don’t.

### Chapter 15: The Lower City: The Cauldron of Commerce

The Lower City is the true heart of Baldur's Gate—a chaotic, energetic, and dangerous crescent of humanity pressed between the bluff of the Upper City and the waters of the Gray Harbour. 60 This is where the city's real work gets done. It is a maze of workshops, crowded tenements, raucous taverns, and bustling marketplaces. Crime is rampant, but so is opportunity. The law is a tangled mess of jurisdictions between the Flaming Fist (at the gates), the Guild Wardens (on their members), the Kontor Guards (in their enclaves), and The Guild's Ward Bosses (everywhere else).

#### Brampton

**Overview.**  
Tucked into the Lower City’s far eastern crook, Brampton is where the map frays and the rules do, too. Legitimate trade rarely bothers with its cramped lanes; that suits Brampton’s residents just fine. The neighborhood’s reputation—grimy, insular, and convenient for anything you don’t want inspected at the Basilisk Gate—keeps rents low and questions lower.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Narrow brick alleys beaded with laundry lines, shuttered “storehouses” with fresh wheel ruts at odd hours, and corner shrines where folk leave copper and apologies. You’ll hear handcarts before you see them, and you’ll smell lamp oil, smoked fish, and damp stone all day long.

**Notable stops.**

- **Hidden Warehouses.** “Abandoned” by daylight, these brick shells reveal false floors, swing-walls, and soot-black hoists by lanternlight. The smartest never keep the same cache twice.  
- **The Salt-Cellar.** A low-beamed tavern that changes proprietors and passwords every season. The chowder is honest; the back room is not.  
- **The Sootwell Steps.** A slant of wet stone dropping beneath a cooper’s yard to an old service tunnel. Locals swear the Steps “never quite end,” which is either a warning or an invitation.  
- **Tally Court.** An open square where gray-market factors meet “between ledgers.” Watch for chalk marks on posts indicating which crews hold the peace that day.

**Who lives here.**  
Cartmen, coopers, sweepers, a few luckless apprentices, and far more “clerks” than any honest census would admit. People remember faces here and forget names on purpose.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Walk with a purpose. Don’t gawk at shuttered doors or count crates aloud. If someone asks who you’re seeing, have a name ready—even if it’s a bluff.

**Local pressures.**  
Smuggling routes contested by Guild crews and would-be independents; Flaming Fist sweeps that seize little but break plenty; and occasional fires that “start themselves” on properties whose owners snub the local Ward Boss.

#### Eastway

**Overview.**  
Eastway fans out from the Basilisk Gate like a wake from a prow. It is the Lower City’s hello and goodbye: a hard-working belt of inns, stables, and porters catering to caravans rolling in from the Coast Way. The crowd changes by the hour; the racket does not.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Gate bells, stable boys sprinting with nosebags, and the steady chant of prices: beds by the head, hay by the bale, storage by the day. The faces are a churn—teamsters, pilgrims, hopefuls—while the locals can spot a newcomer from two streets away.

**Notable stops.**

- **Basilisk Gate Barracks & Heapside Prison.** Tolls, papers, inspections—then cells for those whose luck or tempers fail. The barracks courtyard posts road notices and bounty bills at dawn.  
- **The Singing Lute.** A bright, busy inn where caravan masters hire guards and bards trade routes for verses. Its stew is famous; its back room is discreet.  
- **Tallgate Stables.** Honest hay, decent farriers, and a wry owner who’s seen every scam twice. Expect a queue at sunset.  
- **Porters’ Stone.** A scarred granite block serving as the informal hiring hall. Foremen chalk the day’s rates; muscle lines up with hooks and belts.

**Who lives here.**  
Innkeepers, wheelwrights, quick-fingered guides, shrine-tenders who bless travelers, and pickpockets who don’t. Many families rent rooms above their shops and sleep in shifts during festival weeks.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Keep your purse in front and your papers handy. Haggle, but don’t waste time—someone behind you will pay the posted rate. Peace-tie weapons when the Watch asks; it speeds everything.

**Local pressures.**  
Confidence games targeting first-timers, caravan theft rings testing stables after midnight, and tension every time tolls rise faster than wages. When the road is bad or the rumors worse, Eastway’s smiles thin with the profits.

#### Heapside

**Overview.**  
Heapside runs along the Lower City’s northeast, a neighborhood of honest noise and honest work. Workshops spill sparks, hawkers call from stoops, and the lanes smell of fresh bread, hot iron, and ink. If the Lower City has a conscience, it lives here—alongside a very practical sense of survival.

**What you’ll notice.**  
A patchwork of tenements and guildhalls stitched with washing lines; apprentices jogging for parts; Ilmatari handing out bread at dusk; and street corners that turn into debating societies whenever a crier reads the news.

**Notable stops.**

- **Sorcerous Sundries (The Sorcerous Sodality).** The many-domed landmark where licensed magic is bought, warded, and argued over. Expect apprentices everywhere and a posted schedule of “acceptable pyrotechnics.”  
- **Baldur’s Mouth Printshop.** Five narrow stories of gears, galleys, and gossip. Broadsheets fly at first light; rumors take wing even earlier.  
- **Shrine of the Suffering (Ilmater).** A plain doorstep ministry that never seems to run out of bandages, soup, or patience. Volunteers know every family by name.  
- **The Winch & Whistle.** A corner tavern favored by smiths and scribes alike; tools lean by the hearth, and the chalkboard menu changes with whoever could afford ingredients that day.

**Who lives here.**  
Crafters, carters, scribes, lamplighters, and three generations sharing the same narrow stair. Heapsiders argue politics at breakfast and prices at lunch—and still help you move a chest at dusk.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Pay the going rate, return borrowed tools, and don’t block a lane with idle talk—someone is always hauling something heavier than you. If a neighbor asks you to “mind the pot,” do it.

**Local pressures.**  
Rents creeping up, guild consolidation squeezing independents, and the occasional sealed cellar best left that way after the Absolute Crisis. The Guild keeps a light but real touch; when turf lines blur, fists follow.

#### The Steeps

**Overview.**  
The Steeps climb from the wharves toward the Old Wall in a switchback of stone stairs, terraces, and shopfronts that seem to cling to the hillside by stubbornness alone. This is the Lower City’s “high street,” where porters groan uphill with crates at dawn and well-dressed buyers drift down after luncheon to spend without breaking a sweat.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Narrow lanes punctuated by landings and railings; lamplighters at every bell; clerks in ink-stained cuffs darting between countinghouses; and porters who know the quickest stair for every load. Look up—signboards hang like flags, and many windows are actually glass.

**Notable stops.**

- **Old Baldur’s Gate.** The namesake arch in the Old Wall caps the climb. By day it’s a crush of permits and porters (expect a **5 cp** passage fee); by night, the doors close and the Watch’s lanterns flare.  
- **Felogyr’s Fireworks.** Celebratory rockets out front; serious alchemy in back. The shop is half festival, half arsenal—ask carefully.  
- **Guild Offices & Factors’ Lofts.** Discreet doorplates, locked ledgers, and the occasional auction behind drawn curtains.  
- **The Stair Market.** A string of steep-step stalls: paper goods, ribbon, traveling inks, and surprisingly good pies passed hand to hand.

**Who lives here.**  
Successful shopkeepers, journeymen on the rise, and a few Upper City families who like their luxuries close to the boats. Servants commute; porters never stop.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Keep right on stairs, tip your porter, and don’t argue with a lamplighter’s schedule. If a runner calls “heads,” flatten to a wall—the crate is coming through.

**Local pressures.**  
Class friction (Bloomridge fashions price out longtime tenants), protection “fees” dressed up as night watch contracts, and pickpockets who prefer respectable marks over rough ones. When a fire starts here, it runs uphill fast.

#### Bloomridge

**Overview.**  
Perched for views over Gray Harbour, Bloomridge is fashionable without trying too hard—color-washed townhouses with rooftop gardens, tidy balconies, and salons where trends are invented over citrus wine. The money here is merchant-born and proud of it.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Terraced streets with bay windows and tiled eaves; flower boxes in riotous color; delivery boys balancing hatboxes; and a steady drift of minstrels, perfumers, and personal tailors touting on the quiet.

**Notable stops.**

- **Facemaker’s Boutique.** Figaro “Facemaker” Pennygood cuts cloth and social anxieties in equal measure; fittings are appointments and performances both.  
- **The Bibliophile.** Nansi Gretta’s bookshop smells of leather and peppermints. The back table is where information changes hands, politely.  
- **Bonecloak’s Apothecary & Crimson Draughts.** Twin emporia for remedies, reagents, and respectable vices; everything labeled, nothing entirely innocent.  
- **Outlook Promenade.** A pocket park along the ridge with benches for harbor-watching and gossiping without witnesses.

**Who lives here.**  
Prosperous factors, kontor agents, retired ship captains, and artisans who made it big. Many homes have staff quarters tucked under the eaves.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Dress presentably (clean boots count), book before you browse in the top shops, and let the shopkeeper steer the conversation—especially if you want the *real* catalogue.

**Local pressures.**  
Petty status wars, rising rents that push tradespeople downhill, and “Dandies” (well-bred bravo crews) who sometimes mistake neighborhood pride for license. The Guild prefers velvet gloves here, but the iron is real.

#### Seatower

**Overview.**  
Seatower wraps the Lower City’s western spit of land facing the river mouth. It beats to military time: reveille horns, ship bells, and the clatter of drill squares. On paydays the streets fill with sailors and Fist mercenaries; on storm days, with ropes, tar, and oaths strong enough to season timber.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Tide-rusted chains, stacked shot, and banners snapping over the causeway. Smithies burn hot from dawn; armorers’ courtyards ring like anvils; and quartermasters bark lists faster than scribes can ink them.

**Notable stops.**

- **Seatower of Balduran.** The fortress on its rock, connected by a stout causeway. Access is restricted; the view from the public quay is not.  
- **Forge of the Nine.** Dammon’s workshop turns out quality steel for those who can afford it—and repairs that keep crews alive when they can’t.  
- **Stormshore Armoury.** A rival shop with parade-polish finishes and contracts to match; if it isn’t on the rack, it can be by next watch.  
- **The Chain Locker.** A tar-and-ale tavern where shore leave starts, ends, and sometimes ends up in the brig. The stew is better than the fights.

**Who lives here.**  
Flaming Fist regulars, shipwrights, dock-toughs, sailmakers, and families who measure days by tides. A handful of ministries tend to the wounded and the unlucky.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Don’t block a gangplank or a drill route. Present papers if asked, peace-tie blades near the causeway, and keep clear of powder stores without an escort.

**Local pressures.**  
Shore-leave brawls, munitions smuggling, salvage-rights disputes after storms, and the occasional payroll heist rumor that makes every sergeant count the chest twice. When the fleet’s in, tempers and purses both run light.

#### Gray Harbour (The Dock Ward)

**Overview.**  
Gray Harbour is the Lower City’s working waterfront—piers bristling with masts, winches clacking, and foremen shouting schedules over gulls and surf. If coin has a tide, it rises and falls here. Ships from Amn to Icewind Dale unload at dawn, deals are struck by noon, and by dusk the taverns are louder than the cranes.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Tar, salt, and fish in the air; chalked cargo marks on every crate; stevedores moving with practiced rhythm; Kontor guards in foreign colors watching their warehouse doors; and the steady ring of shipwrights refitting hulls between voyages.

**Notable stops.**

- **The Blushing Mermaid.** A notorious, barnacled tavern partly housed in the ribs of an old hulk. Fights are common; information is cheaper than the beer and twice as strong.  
- **The Counting House.** The port’s private bank and clearinghouse. Factors queue with ledgers; bodyguards wait with blank expressions. If you’re moving serious cargo or credit, you will end up here.  
- **Water Queen’s House (Umberlee).** Sailors pay the Bitch Queen in coin or trinkets before casting off. The stairs are always wet, as if the tide climbs to hear the prayers.  
- **Foreign Kontors.** Walled compounds for Amn, Waterdeep, Sembia, and Lantan. Inside: their own guards, scales, and disputes. Outside: hawkers who know exactly what each nation misses from home.  
- **Steel Watch Foundry.** A shuttered, iron-boned factory from Gortash’s heyday. The Fist keeps a cordon around it; scavengers keep finding new ways in and worse reasons to regret it.

**Who lives here.**  
Dockworkers, net-menders, sailmakers, chandlers, a dozen breeds of clerk, and families who measure years in successful returns to port.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Don’t stand on a gangplank, don’t argue with a crane signalman, and don’t short a union crew on their fee. If a sailor says “one for Umberlee,” follow suit and toss a coin—the sea has a long memory.

**Local pressures.**  
Smuggling threaded through legitimate manifests, Kontor politics that spill into alley brawls, warehouse fires that start “by themselves,” and periodic strikes when wages lag the risk.

### Chapter 16: The Outer City: The Unwalled Wilds

The Outer City is not a single district but a vast, chaotic sprawl of settlements that have grown beyond the taxable jurisdiction of the main walls. It is a place of refugees, transients, exiles, and those too poor or too independent to live within the Gate proper. Law is scarce, and life is cheap. 61

#### New Elturel

**Overview.**  
Born of catastrophe and stubborn hope, New Elturel is a sprawling refugee borough of tents, timber shacks, and newly raised chapels on the south bank of the Chionthar. Its people are survivors first and citizens second—Hellriders on half pay, families clutching heirlooms, and priests who learned to triage faith and fever.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Order woven from scarcity: neat rows of tents, communal cookfires, water wagons making scheduled rounds, and watchposts flying sunburst pennants. At dusk, hymn-songs carry over the river like a promise.

**Notable stops.**

- **The High Rider’s Stand.** An open-air pavilion that serves as militia headquarters and council hall. Rosters are posted at dawn; scouts report river news at dusk.  
- **The Sun-Blessed Chapel (Torm, Ilmater, Lathander).** Part sanctuary, part soup kitchen, part field hospital. Services are plain; the lines are long; the comfort is real.  
- **Dawnmarket.** A refugee-run bazaar where farm tools, salvaged books, and baked “Last Light” loaves trade hands. Prices are fair by custom, not by law.  
- **Memorial Row.** A lane of carved wooden pillars etched with names that never made it home. Visitors leave white ribbons; locals leave a moment of silence.  
- **River Steps.** Terraced banks used for washing, fishing, and ferry musters to Rivington. Notice boards here carry day labor and escort postings for trips into the city.

**Who lives here.**  
Elturian families, Hellrider veterans and trainees, Ilmatari volunteers, and artisans rebuilding skills into livelihoods—leatherworkers, bakers, and smiths setting up honest stalls.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Ask before offering coin (some take offense at charity, none refuse paid work). Don’t mock uniforms held together by faith and thread. If you’re Baldurian, lead with respect; politics can wait.

**Local pressures.**  
Supply gaps, riverborne sickness after floods, friction with nearby traders over prices and permits, and Flaming Fist “security visits” that sometimes look like recruitment and sometimes like reminders of who holds the bridge.

#### Rivington

**Overview.**  
Rivington is the south-bank gateway to Baldur’s Gate: caravan yards, cheap inns, bustling stables, and markets that never quite close. Think of it as the city’s foyer—less polished than the rooms beyond, but far friendlier if you arrive dusty and hungry.

**What you’ll notice.**  
A blur of road dust and river mist; innkeepers calling room rates; stablehands jogging nosebags; street vendors hawking skewers to travelers threading for the bridge; and Flaming Fist checkpoints that can be brisk or unbearable, depending on the hour.

**Notable stops.**

- **Open Hand Temple (Ilmater).** Refuge, bread, and a quiet corner to catch your breath. The notice board out front is the best place in Rivington to find honest work quickly.  
- **Circus of the Last Days.** Lucretious’s traveling wonder—planar oddities, knife acts, and stories that last longer than their ticket stubs. Pop-up, seasonal, and impossible to miss when it’s in.  
- **Arfur’s Mansion.** A prominent toy-maker’s home and workshop with a cheery façade and nervous shutters. Locals have stories; few match.  
- **Southbank Market.** A sprawl of canvas-roofed stalls: tack, travel rations, second-hand armor, and roadside cures of arguable merit.  
- **Bridgefoot Commons.** The square before the ascent to Wyrm’s Crossing. Porters gather here at first bell; rumor peddlers at last light.

**Who lives here.**  
Innkeepers, stable owners, drovers, day-laborers, a few retired caravan guards, and families who make their living feeding the road.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Peace-tie blades near the checkpoints, keep travel papers handy, and tip your porter before you need him again. Bargain, but don’t haggle a family’s supper into thin air.

**Local pressures.**  
Toll hikes squeezing margins, smuggling routes that skirt the bridge under cover of ferries, pickpocket crews hunting festival days, and periodic crackdowns that punish the honest and miss the clever.

#### Wyrm's Crossing

**Overview.**  
Part bridge, part neighborhood, Wyrm’s Crossing strides the Chionthar on two great spans that meet at **Wyrm’s Rock Fortress**. Shops and tenements are built right into the stonework; from dawn to curfew the roadway is a rolling caravan of carts, pilgrims, and news. The Flaming Fist runs the drawbridges, the tolls, and the temper of the place.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Bells from the fortress timing openings; toll officers with tablets; hawkers threading traffic with skewers and hand pies; laundry lines strung between upper galleries; and a constant thrum of wheel, hoof, and rumor.

**Notable stops.**

- **Wyrm’s Rock Fortress.** The Flaming Fist’s bridgehead—permits checked, disputes settled, and drawbridges lifted with impressive indifference to your schedule. Public areas post notices and river conditions.  
- **Sharess’ Caress.** A festhall-inn with velvet manners and surprisingly good security. Whispered conversations in curtained booths often matter more than the entertainment.  
- **Danthelon’s Dancing Axe.** A quality arms shop with an immaculate reputation and a quiet back room where the right kind of questions sometimes find answers.  
- **Fraygo’s Flophouse.** The cheapest pillow on the bridge and the loudest snores in the city. Bring your own lock and a sense of humor.  
- **Underbridge Piers.** Steps down to boat landings used by ferries, fishermen, and—on foggy nights—people who prefer not to cross at street level.

**Who lives here.**  
Innkeepers, stall vendors, toll clerks, off-duty Fist, scribes-for-hire, a few families who’ve held lofts since before the last repair, and a floating population between jobs on either bank.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Have coin ready, peace-tie weapons when asked, keep to the left if you’re on foot, and do not block a drawbridge lane—fines are instant. Expect bag checks during high alert.

**Local pressures.**  
Toll hikes, protection “offers” to shopkeepers, pickpocket crews working festival days, and smuggling runs that slip under the spans by night. When the bridge locks down, the whole south bank feels it.

#### Twin Songs

**Overview.**  
A borough of shrines and small halls, Twin Songs hosts devotions to more gods than any guide can list. Faiths coexist here by custom and caution; services overlap like chimes in a breeze. Outsiders come for blessings, locals come for belonging.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Prayer flags on eaves, incense coiling from doorways, bell chimes at all hours, and a patchwork of processions: Ilmatari soup lines beside moonlit hymns to Selûne; a debate on doctrine two doors down from a bake sale.

**Notable stops.**

- **Church of Last Hope (Ilmater, Lathander, Torm).** Shared altar, shared kitchen, and shared burdens. Refuge, bandages, and counsel without judgment.  
- **Shrine Row (Siger’s Walk).** A lane dense with pocket shrines—coins for Tymora, candles for Helm, a silvered skull for Jergal. If you can’t find your patron here, your patron may be shy.  
- **The Open Secret.** An unmarked hall where controversial faiths meet under strict house rules: no rites that spill into the street, no recruiters at the door. Locals pretend not to know it; everyone knows it.  
- **Pilgrims’ Canteen.** A modest refectory staffed by volunteers from three temples; the stew changes daily and the bulletin board changes lives.  
- **Bellwater Steps.** Terraced river stairs for ablutions and candle-float rites at dusk—solemn, beautiful, and a favorite spot for quiet talks.

**Who lives here.**  
Lay ministers, mendicants, retired temple guards, families who prize community over coin, and refugees who found in faith what the city would not sell.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Peace-tie blades, remove helms indoors, and ask before photographing (or sketching) sacred art. A small donation smooths most introductions; respect smooths the rest.

**Local pressures.**  
Tension between tolerant neighbors and intolerant visitors; relic thefts that temples would rather not publicize; and periodic raids by zealots who mistake coexistence for weakness.

#### Sow’s Foot

**Overview.**  
Sow’s Foot is Baldur’s Gate in miniature turned up to full volume: expatriate cookfires, street theatres, prayer tents, animal pens, and market rows that reinvent themselves every week. If the city has a flavor you’ve never tasted, you’ll find it here sizzling on a brazier.

**What you’ll notice.**  
A chorus of languages; smoke from three continents; drummers practicing beside a bookseller’s tarp; kids racing goats down an alley; and Flaming Fist patrols that prefer to keep moving.

**Notable stops.**

- **Hamhock’s Slaughterhouse.** A sprawling abattoir with a grim sense of humor and reliable prices. Supplies half the south bank and—so rumor insists—the occasional quiet favor.  
- **Pavilion of Plenty.** The heart of the market: spice cones, desert teas, beadwork, second-hand armor, and street food from places your map forgot. Lanterns make it a festival after dark.  
- **Menagerie of Many.** Exotic beasts, odd companions, and a showman who swears every cage is double-latched. You will believe him until a latch proves single.  
- **Council Tent.** An ever-moving canvas hall where elders of different communities meet to settle disputes and wrangle with city envoys. Spectators welcome if silent.  
- **Cistern Twelve.** An old stone tank now ringed with laundry lines and water sellers; it’s the best place to hear tomorrow’s rumors today.

**Who lives here.**  
Rashemi fur traders, Shou puppeteers, Calishite spice merchants, lizardfolk tanners, halfling bakers, and human families from everywhere in between. Outsiders to the Gate, neighbors to each other.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Ask before photographing beasts or people, bargain with a smile, and do not insult a cooking pot you haven’t tried. Tip performers; mind your purse; follow stallholders’ cues in crowd surges.

**Local pressures.**  
Chronic water and waste problems, price-gouging by opportunists, flare-ups between proud traditions, and Guild crews edging in on market security. When disease sweeps the south bank, Sow’s Foot catches it first and fights it hardest.

#### Whitkeep

**Overview.**  
Whitkeep is a pocket borough of gnome ingenuity stitched to leather and dye. White-washed walls, a crumbling manor, and a ring of tanneries give it a distinct look—and smell. Artists, tinkerers, and radicals share lanes with vat-men and hide-haulers; somehow, it works. If you’re hunting clever devices, street murals, or a debate that ends in a potluck, start here.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Color splashed on plaster; wind-spun mobiles of brass and glass; clacking contraptions trundling between workshops; and the ever-present tang of curing vats. Fiddle tunes drift from open doors, punctuated by the occasional “Don’t touch that\!”

**Notable stops.**

- **Whitkeep Manor.** Once a minor estate, now a communal hall and dormitories for gnome families. Evening unveilings of new inventions are equal parts science fair and social hour.  
- **Greenjack Tannery.** The largest local yard; tours by arrangement, boots required. Good for hardwearing leather and overheard union talk.  
- **The Brass Brow Café.** Strong coffee, stronger opinions. Morning design critiques, afternoon pamphleteering, and the city’s most civil arguments.  
- **Maker’s Lane.** A row of open-front workshops selling clockwork novelties, clever locks, collapsible poles, and other “adventurer helpers” field-tested by the makers themselves.  
- **Stacks & Scraps.** A salvage yard of sorted curiosities: gears, hinges, bent tools, and the odd “mystery part” priced by charm and risk. Bring gloves.

**Who lives here.**  
Rock gnomes and their in-laws, journeyman dyers, apprentice artificers, a handful of students who never left, and families who prize independence over polish.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Ask before handling a device; illusions are common and not a prank unless labeled so. Do not sneer at the smell—someone’s mortgage depends on it. If a bucket brigade runs by, step in or step aside.

**Local pressures.**  
Workshop fires, dye spills, and periodic friction between tanners and artists over noise, runoff, and festivals. Flaming Fist visits spike after protests; the Guild sniffs around during boomlets, angling for “protection” contracts that most locals prefer to negotiate as a neighborhood, not as victims.

#### Norchapel

**Overview.**  
Encircling Little Calimshan like a belt of cottages and shopfronts, Norchapel is tidy, devout, and practical. Residents pool coin for extra watch rounds, keep shrines swept, and prefer disputes settled at a hall table rather than in the street. Compared to its louder neighbors, this is the Outer City at a gentle hum.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Lanterns lit early, candles glowing in wall-niches, swept stoops, and wardens making calm, predictable circuits. Signboards advertise bakers, cobblers, chandleries, and “repairs while you wait.”

**Notable stops.**

- **The Quiet Jacks’ Hall.** A rebuilt stone chapel turned community house and de-facto security office. Disputes mediated daily; troublemakers *escorted* elsewhere.  
- **Marchel’s Garden Market (Godsday).** Produce, fresh cheeses, and flowers laid out on trestles under old trees; cooks from the Upper City sometimes shop in disguise.  
- **Watchful Nest Shrines.** Dozens of tiny alcoves honoring Helm, Torm, and household guardians; locals keep the candles burning from dusk to dawn.  
- **Candle-Menders’ Row.** Waxworks and wick sellers; bring your own jar for discounted tapers.  
- **Boundary Walk.** The circuit path along Little Calimshan’s wall is popular for evening strolls and quiet talk; guards on both sides prefer it peaceful.

**Who lives here.**  
Craftspeople, shopkeepers, halfling gardeners, Elturian families rebuilding routines, and elders who remember when this was proper countryside.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Lower your voice after evening bells, keep to the right on narrow lanes, and don’t sermonize at shrines you don’t keep. If you benefit from the extra patrols, contribute to the lantern fund when asked.

**Local pressures.**  
“Safety fees” edging toward extortion when times are lean; tension with newcomers who can’t pay yet; and the constant worry that a heavy-handed Watch action will upend the neighborhood’s carefully negotiated calm with Little Calimshan.

#### Little Calimshan

**Overview.**  
Behind high mud-brick walls set with bottle-glass, Little Calimshan preserves a slice of the south: courtyards, fountains, saffron steam, and oud music under lanterns. The gates open on a timetable and close on trouble; inside, elders guard tradition while traders count coin. It is both welcoming and watchful.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Colorful awnings, patterned tiles, spices on the air, and orderly queues at gate hours. Children perched on the wall at sunset; guards with curved blades at street corners who nod if you mind your manners.

**Notable stops.**

- **Calim Jewel Emporium.** Courtyard showcases of filigree and fire-cut stones, appraised by a matriarch whose gaze weighs more than the scales. Commissions by appointment.  
- **The Oasis.** A tea-house built around an ever-flowing fountain; business over cardamom in daylight, stories and song after dark.  
- **Sun Gate.** The main gate and customs post; visiting hours are posted, and papers are checked with courtesy and care.  
- **Copper Bazaar.** A market square for spices, textiles, oils, and festival sweets; haggling is expected, insults are not.  
- **House of Stories.** An elders’ courtyard where travelers’ tales and family histories are traded for tea. Visitors are welcome if they listen more than they speak.  
- **Loomstreet.** Tailors and weavers working in shaded arcades; wedding cloth, traveling silks, and hard-wearing caravan gear cut to fit.

**Who lives here.**  
Calishite families, jewelers and gem-cutters, spice sellers, caravan agents, young people walking the line between two homes, and community guards who know every face by name.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Enter during posted hours or by invitation. Greet with a hand over the heart; accept tea if offered; keep hands off wares until invited. Dress modestly, speak plainly, and do not photograph (or sketch) people without permission.

**Local pressures.**  
Generational debates over how open to be with outsiders, occasional friction with nearby refugee markets, outside prejudice met with inside solidarity, and quiet tug-of-war between community leaders and a local Guild crew that prefers to be helpful—for a percentage.

#### Stonyeyes

**Overview.**  
Stonyeyes is the city’s livestock belly—corrals, pens, and muddy lanes where drovers, half-orc porters, and stable crews keep Baldur’s Gate fed and moving. Named for the weathered gargoyles over an old gatehouse (locals swear they were once basilisk victims), the district wakes before dawn and doesn’t truly sleep.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Bleating, lowing, and auction shouts; straw underfoot; whip-smart handlers moving herds between chalked sale rings; tack shops with harness displays out front; and a constant barter of coin, salt, and favors. Smiles are rare but handshakes are binding.

**Notable stops.**

- **Eastway Stockyards.** The main corrals, partitioned by species and temperament. Market days get loud; bring boots and a sense of direction.  
- **The Half-Hoof Tavern.** Part hiring hall, part breakfast counter. Foremen post day rates on a nail-board; porridge comes thick enough to stand a spoon.  
- **Creaky House.** A notorious boarding house for hands between jobs. Cheap bunks, stricter rules, and a roof that complains in high wind.  
- **Drovers’ Lane.** Farriers, tack-menders, salt sellers, and a nimble-fingered saddler who can repair a strap while you finish a tankard.

**Who lives here.**  
Half-orc porters prized for strength and fairness, drovers and breeders from the hinterlands, stablehands with rope-burned palms, and families who time supper to auction bells.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Ask before touching animals, tip handlers who save your boots, and step aside when you hear “Drive\!”—it’s not a suggestion. Bargain firmly, pay promptly.

**Local pressures.**  
Injuries, disease outbreaks after a bad rain, “lost” stock that usually isn’t, and periodic Guild interest in who guards which pens. When prices spike, tempers do, too.

#### Blackgate

**Overview.**  
Blackgate is the Trade Way’s doorstep: caravan circles, smoke from smithies, and yards loud with harness jingles. Most travelers see it before they see the city, and many spend more time (and coin) here than they planned. It is bustling, opportunistic, and very good at separating the unprepared from their purses.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Wagon wheels on packed gravel, shouted inventories, stable steam in the morning chill, and signboards advertising everything from axle repairs to “honest guards with references.” The skyline is low except for the **Black Dragon Gate**—a constant frame for comings and goings.

**Notable stops.**

- **Black Dragon Gate.** Toll checks, permit stamps, and a notice wall layered with jobs, bounties, and warnings that no one reads until they need to.  
- **Stables Row.** A long run of paddocks and barns. **Gelduth’s Rest** anchors the strip with decent straw for beasts and indifferent ale for drovers.  
- **Ironhands’ Foundry.** Shield-dwarf smiths whose wagon gear almost never fails and whose tempers never quite cool. Expect a queue and fair prices.  
- **The Circle Grounds.** A trampled field where caravans form rings at night. Firelight, dice games, and the best place to find last-minute hires.

**Who lives here.**  
Teamsters, wheelwrights, hostlers, smiths, jewel-fingered factors who “happen” to be passing through, and locals who rent everything from haylofts to spots by a warm wall.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Don’t block a forge door, don’t insult a smith, and don’t follow strangers to a “quiet place” to change coins. If a caravan captain vouches you, doors open; otherwise, expect to wait your turn.

**Local pressures.**  
Con artists, protection rackets that come with actual protection, and Fist patrols that arrive just late enough to make a point. Prices swing with the season; so does patience.

#### The K'liir

**Overview.**  
Raised after the Absolute Crisis by githyanki who chose the Gate over the Astral, the K’liir is a disciplined enclave of angular tents, psionically braced gantries, and pale stone spires that look grown rather than built. It is part drill yard, part embassy, and entirely itself.

**What you’ll notice.**  
Geometric pennants that barely move in the wind, silent formations running spear drills, and a low crystalline hum at the center of camp that seems to fade if you listen too hard. Visitors are watched, not welcomed or shunned—assessed.

**Notable stops.**

- **The Proving Ground.** A warded plaza where martial and psionic forms are practiced in perfect cadence. Observers are permitted along marked lines; crossing them ends your tour.  
- **Psionic Resonator.** A faceted crystal obelisk used for long-range communion and scrying. It thrums like a distant hive; guides ask for silence nearby.  
- **The Concord Pavilion.** A canvas-and-bone hall where emissaries parley. Appointments only; translated courtesy is the rule.  
- **Astralwrights’ Shed.** A spartan stall trading in githyanki-forged steel, void-cured leathers, and curious climbing gear prized by daring locals.

**Who lives here.**  
Orpheus-loyal veterans, an austere cadre of trainers, a handful of Sword Coast intermediaries, and a small cohort of Gate-born youths learning gith forms with careful permission.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Announce yourself at the perimeter post; wait to be acknowledged. Do not mimic salutes or practice forms uninvited. Sheath steel, shield thoughts if you can, and accept that questions will be answered with fewer words than you asked.

**Local pressures.**  
Tense politics with Upper City skeptics and Outer City gawkers, theft attempts by collectors chasing “astral curios,” and occasional feuds with mercenaries who mistake restraint for weakness. The K’liir answers slights as a unit.

#### Tumbledown & The Floodway

**Overview.**  
Beyond the river bluffs, cemeteries and stonemasons’ yards spread like a quiet city of their own. Tumbledown’s grief is orderly: funerary trades, carvers’ sheds, and sober processions. The Floodway below is the opposite—low ground of tilting tenements and boardwalk lanes that drown after a hard rain and rise again, stubborn as reeds.

**What you’ll notice.**  
In Tumbledown: wind through grave cypress, chisel taps from dawn to dusk, and stone markers stacked like books in a patient library. In the Floodway: laundry lines above knee-deep streets, plankwalks over brown water, and children who can pole a skiff before they can write.

**Notable stops.**

- **Candulhallow’s Tombstones.** The best-known carvers’ yard, equal parts workshop and open-air gallery. Epitaphs range from poetry to practical warnings.  
- **Cliffgate.** Fog-wreathed gate linking the bluffs to the Lower City. The drop beside the road is sheer; the reputation for muggings is earned.  
- **The Lych-Steps.** Old stone stairs descending toward the Floodway, used for processions in better weather and for quick exits in worse.  
- **Highwater Wharf.** A raised dock for skiffs and ferries when the Floodway lives up to its name; rumor boards here post missing-person notes after storms.  
- **Stonecutters’ Row.** Tool shops, stencil sellers, and a mason-priests’ refectory that serves hearty noon meals to anyone who worked a morning.

**Who lives here.**  
Morticians, stonecutters, gravediggers, quiet families with steady hands, and, down in the Floodway, laborers who can’t afford higher ground but will not leave their neighbors.

**Etiquette & access.**  
Keep voices low among graves, don’t lean on markers, and step wide for processions. In the Floodway, ask a local before trying a side path—today’s dry plank was yesterday’s plank boat.

**Local pressures.**  
Grave robbers, sinkholes after long rains, tenants displaced by the third flood in a month, and the occasional “restless dead” report the Row shrugs off until a priest stops shrugging. In both halves of the district, respect is the currency that keeps the peace.

### Chapter 17: The Undercity & The Undercellar

Beneath the streets of Baldur's Gate lies a city in darkness. These are not one and the same, though they are often confused by surface-dwellers.

* **The Undercity:** This is the common term for the city's sewer network, a labyrinth of tunnels, cisterns, and forgotten crypts. It is a dangerous place, home to monstrous creatures and outcasts. It is also the primary domain of The Guild, which uses the sewers as a highway for smuggling and maintains its main, heavily-defended **Guildhall** in a secret, fortified sewer nexus. 58  
* **The Undercellar:** Specifically located beneath The Wide in the Upper City, the Undercellar is a network of ancient, vaulted cellars. It serves as a clandestine marketplace where the city's elite can purchase illegal goods—rare poisons, forbidden scrolls, slaves—delivered by The Guild through secret tunnels that bypass the city's walls entirely.

# The Masquerade Rule

Across this campaign, *who you appear to be* is mechanically and politically distinct from *who you are*. Personas are not deception layered atop a true self; they are functional, often magical, masks one wears to act in the world. This is the principle that makes the Green Masquerade make sense — but it extends well beyond that society.

## Three corollaries

1. **Performance is power.** A convincing role *grants* access. Mister Smiles' troupe doesn't just look like clowns; the performance itself is the magic that harvests emotion. The Green Masquerade's seasonal balls are not parties, they are *rituals* whose form does the work.
2. **Identity is currency.** A face seen, a true name spoken, a mask removed — each transfers value. Masquerade members never reveal their true face at gatherings; the taboo is so old it carries an automatic curse. To be *known* is to be *spent*.
3. **Masks both protect and bind.** A mask shields the wearer's name from fey leverage (see [fey-bargains.md](fey-bargains.md)) — but the mask itself accumulates a piece of the wearer's spirit over time. The protection is a slow, willing exchange.

## Applied to the campaign

- A PC who builds a notorious public persona has a usable *handle* on the world — and a vulnerability if that persona is unmasked at the wrong moment.
- Social scenes among the elite (Waterdhavian salons, patriar dinners, Carnival shows) should be played as *ritual*: the form matters, deviation has cost.
- An NPC's mask-name (Lord Fox, Lady Duskrose, Mister Smiles) is the only handle most people have on them. Learning a true name is leverage worth as much as gold.
- Mass unmaskings — at a ball, at a riot, on the front page of *Baldur's Mouth* — are climactic events, not throwaway beats.

## Where it shows up locally

Baldur's Gate has no Green Masquerade chapter, but the principle is everywhere:

- The **Carnival of Whispers** runs on it (the ringmaster is the role; Mister Smiles is the mask).
- **Patriar society** runs on it (heritage names, sigils, hereditary seats — the persona inherits the office, not the person).
- **Worshipful Companies** run on it (a guildmaster's regalia *is* the office for the term they hold it).

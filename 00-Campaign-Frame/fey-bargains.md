# Fey Bargains

Fey-pact behavior in this campaign follows a small set of consistent rules, distilled from the Green Masquerade dossier. They apply equally to a hedge-witch trading with a sprite and a Waterdhavian noble striking a deal with the Queen of Air and Darkness.

## The four rules

1. **Favors are the medium of exchange.** Coin and signed contracts are absent or ornamental. Every act of aid is recorded as a *favor* owed, callable at any time. "Better ten small debts than one great one" is the savvy reveler's creed.
2. **Debt is bondage.** A mortal who lets a single archfey hold most of their ledger becomes that fey's *thrall* in all but name. The Carnival of Whispers' Mister Smiles is the cautionary local example: he traded fame for a debt he can never finish paying.
3. **The letter is the law.** Fey honor the *exact* terms of a bargain — and exploit every ambiguity. A promise of "a song at moonrise" is a song at *which* moonrise, where, before whom? Spell out everything; assume the fey will pick the worst reading.
4. **A name is a handle.** Knowing a creature's *true name* — fey or mortal — gives leverage over them in any pact. Masks (see [the-masquerade-rule.md](the-masquerade-rule.md)) exist in part to keep true names unspoken.

## The ledger principle

Because fey are notoriously fickle, the only durable safeguard is an external record. The Green Masquerade keeps a sentient **Ledger of Favors** in Waterdeep; lesser cells keep chapbooks. *A promise written is harder to wriggle out of.* Mortals dealing with the fey without a ledger are gambling.

## Applied to the campaign

- Any NPC with a fey patron can be assumed to owe more favors than they have collected — most are quietly underwater.
- A PC offered a "free" boon by a fey should treat it as a future debt, not a gift.
- Breaking a fey bargain doesn't trigger combat; it triggers *consequence at the worst possible moment*. The fey wait.
- Cold iron, true-sight, and salt circles disrupt fey magic but do not dissolve a bargain already struck.
